## Review: Economic Dispatch (Lecture 2)

### Problem Formulation

$$\min_{g_{s}} \sum_{s} o_{s} g_{s}$$

Subject to:

$$\sum_{s} g_{s} - d = 0 \leftrightarrow \lambda$$

$$-g_{s} + G_{s} \geq 0 \leftrightarrow \overline{\mu_{s}}$$

### Lagrangian

$$\mathcal{L}(g_{s}, \lambda, \overline{\mu_{s}}) = \sum_{s} o_{s} g_{s} - \lambda(\sum_{s} g_{s} - d) - \sum_{s} \overline{\mu_{s}}(-g_{s} + G_{s})$$

### KKT Condition

$$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial g_{s}} = o_{s} - \lambda + \overline{\mu_{s}} = 0$$

### Interpretation of $\lambda$

- $\lambda$ is the **shadow price** of the energy balance constraint
- It represents the cost of supplying one additional MWh, i.e. the **electricity price**
- The market clears at the variable cost of the **marginal generator** (the last one in merit order whose capacity is not binding):

$$\overline{\mu_{s_{1}}^{\ast}} = 0 \Rightarrow o_{s_{1}} - \lambda^{\ast} = 0 \Rightarrow \lambda^{\ast} = o_{s_{1}}$$

## Optimal Generator and Consumer Behaviour

### Generator (price-taker)

A generator maximises profit (revenue minus cost):

$$\max_{g}[\lambda g - C(g)]$$

Optimal condition:

$$\lambda = \frac{\partial C}{\partial g}\bigg|_{g=g^{\ast}}$$

For constant marginal cost $C(g) = o_{s} \cdot g$, this gives $\lambda = o_{s}$.

The marginal cost curve $\frac{\partial C}{\partial g}$ is the **supply curve** of a competitive firm.

Capacity constraint: $g_{s} \leq G_{s} \leftrightarrow \overline{\mu_{s}}$

### Consumer (price-taker)

A consumer maximises net utility:

$$\max_{d}[U(d) - \lambda d]$$

Optimal condition:

$$\lambda = \frac{\partial U}{\partial d}\bigg|_{d=d^{\ast}}$$

The marginal utility curve $\frac{\partial U}{\partial d}$ is the **demand curve** of a rational consumer.

---

## Welfare Maximisation and the First Welfare Theorem

The total welfare (consumer surplus + generator surplus) is maximised where aggregated supply meets aggregated demand:

$$\max_{d_{b}, g_{s}}\left[\sum_{b} U_{b}(d_{b}) - \sum_{s} C_{s}(g_{s})\right]$$

Subject to:

$$\sum_{b} d_{b} - \sum_{s} g_{s} = 0 \leftrightarrow \lambda$$

**First Welfare Theorem**: At the competitive equilibrium, maximising total welfare gives the same result as each agent independently maximising their own welfare. This is the microeconomic justification for electricity markets.

### Simplification: Price-Inelastic Demand

Assuming inelastic demand $D$ (constant), linear utility, and linear costs:

$$\max_{g_{s}}\left[o_{d} D - \sum_{s} o_{s} g_{s}\right]$$

Since $D$ is constant, **maximising welfare is equivalent to minimising total generation cost**.

### Elastic Demand via Dummy Generator

Demand elasticity or load shedding can be represented by a dummy generator $g_{d}$ where $d = D - g_{d}$. This dummy generator has a cost equal to the value of lost load. Grouping it with real generators again shows the equivalence of welfare maximisation and cost minimisation.

## Electricity Markets: Key Features

The electricity market follows standard supply-demand logic, with a few important differences:

- Supply and demand must **balance at every time step**
- Demand is typically **inelastic** in the short run
- The solution must be compatible with **power flow equations** on the network

### Merit-Order Effect

Increasing penetration of renewable generation shifts the supply curve right, reducing the market clearing price. This is the **merit-order effect**.

### Opportunity Cost

Generator behaviour depends not only on marginal cost but also on **opportunity cost**: the value of producing at a different time instead. This is especially relevant for **storage** and **hydro** resources.

$$\text{Opportunity cost} = \text{loss of potential gain from alternatives}$$

## Joint Capacity and Dispatch Optimisation

### Problem Formulation

Optimisation variables are now:
- $g_{s,t}$: energy generated by generator $s$ at time step $t$
- $G_{s}$: installed capacity of generator $s$

$$\min_{g_{s,t}, G_{s}}\left[\sum_{s} c_{s} G_{s} + \sum_{s,t} o_{s} g_{s,t}\right]$$

Subject to:

$$\sum_{s} g_{s,t} - d_{t} = 0 \leftrightarrow \lambda_{t} \quad \text{(energy balance at every } t\text{)}$$

$$-g_{s,t} + G_{s} \geq 0 \leftrightarrow \overline{\mu_{s,t}} \quad \text{(capacity constraint at every } s, t\text{)}$$

Where:
- $c_{s}$ = annualised capital cost of generator $s$ (EUR/MW)
- $o_{s}$ = variable cost of generator $s$ (EUR/MWh)

### KKT: Dispatch Condition

Differentiating the Lagrangian with respect to $g_{s,t}$:

$$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial g_{s,t}} = o_{s} - \lambda_{t}^{\ast} + \overline{\mu_{s,t}^{\ast}} = 0 \Rightarrow \overline{\mu_{s,t}^{\ast}} = \lambda_{t}^{\ast} - o_{s}$$

For a given capacity, the hourly dispatch is the same as single-period economic dispatch. The marginal generator at each hour satisfies:

$$\lambda_{t}^{\ast} = \frac{\partial C_{s}}{\partial g_{s}}\bigg|_{g=g^{\ast}} = o_{s}$$

### KKT: Investment Condition

Differentiating the Lagrangian with respect to $G_{s}$:

$$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial G_{s}} = c_{s} - \sum_{t} \overline{\mu_{s,t}^{\ast}} = 0$$

$$c_{s} = \sum_{t} \overline{\mu_{s,t}^{\ast}} = \sum_{t}(\lambda_{s,t}^{\ast} - o_{s})$$

Or more precisely (since $\overline{\mu_{s,t}^{\ast}} = 0$ when the generator is not dispatched):

$$c_{s} = \sum_{t} \max(0, \lambda_{t}^{\ast} - o_{s})$$

### Economic Interpretation

> The level of investment in a generator's capacity is optimal when the **sum over all hours of the gap between the electricity price and the generator's variable cost** equals the **capital cost**.

This "gap" is the **infra-marginal rent** earned in each hour when the generator is dispatched and the price exceeds its variable cost. Summing these rents over the year must exactly cover the capital cost at the optimum.

The price must exceed $o_{s}$ (the variable cost of the expensive generator) for enough hours that the **area under the price duration curve** above $o_{s}$ equals $c_{s}$.

## High Electricity Prices

High electricity prices are:

- A **feature, not a bug**: they allow expensive (peaking) generators to recover capital costs
- A signal of **scarcity** (renewable droughts, fuel shortages)
- A consequence of **inelastic demand** (could be smoothed by demand response)
- Potentially a sign of **market power** (if perfect competition breaks down)
- Central to the debate on **capacity mechanisms**: how to pay for peaking plants that are rarely used but essential for resilience
